Tuition Going Up, Job Prospects Going Down

Over the past decade tuition has increased significantly. In fact in the past five years, nationwide, it has increased by nearly $1,000. In 2010, undergraduate tuition fees for all students across Canada was $5,146, in the 2014/2015 academic year, average students are paying $5,959, with the most expensive discipline being dentistry in which average students pay $18,187; and the least expensive being education, in which average students pay $4,510. The relatively significant increase converts to a 16 per cent increase over a four year period, which in turn is a roughly 4 per cent growth rate year-over-year during the period. But the more telling discrepancy exists when this rapid increase in tuition is compared to qualitative data such as the number of students finding employment after the have completed their undergraduate studies. There is no direct pool of information to source nationwide averages that illustrate the number of students obtaining employment, but many schools provide graduation and employment rates, such as Ryerson University. At Ryerson, the number of students who are and were able to find employment has decreased, is continuing to decrease and the trend is showing no signs of changing, regardless of program and discipline. For instance, a student graduating from Ryerson’s successful journalism program in 2009 was statistically guaranteed a job in 2 years and 94.3 percent of graduates obtaining employment in six months. It is safe to say these numbers have changed significantly over the past 5 years. Today, according to the numbers it is more likely that a student graduating from the very same program is 91.4 percent likely to find employment, and 81.8 percent of students obtaining employment in 6 months.

Probability of Getting a Job is Lower

According to the Oxford English dictionary, University is defined as a high-level educational institution in which students study for degrees and academic researches done. Students attend university for a multitude of reason, including improving their chance of gaining employment in the field they studied. But in Canada, the likelihood of attaining employment after graduation is becoming more unlikely. In 2009, the number of students who attained employment at Ryerson University in six months, on average, was 94 percent, with 95.9 percent of graduates attaining employment in two years. In addition, increased enrollment has further exacerbated this discrepancy in employment. More and more students are enrolling in university. At Ryerson, in 2009 there were 516 students enrolled in the journalism program, in less than five years that number has increased to just less than 600 to 590 students. This has placed increased pressure on educative capabilities of universities. According to the University of Toronto’s Long Range Budget Guidelines 2013-2014 to 2017-2018 and Budget Report 2013-2014, increased enrollment has exhausted university resources, and has further placed increased pressure on the universities educative resources: “Enrolment is the primary driver of operating revenue, generating 87% of total university operating revenues. The University has carefully managed enrolment growth, spreading it across the three campuses and most faculties. The corresponding increase in revenue has helped mitigate the impact of the stagnation of growth in provincial per-student grant funding relative to inflation. But increased enrolments have exacerbated pressure on faculty-student ratios and on student space. Indeed, growth has been halted in some quarters of the University, due to these pressures and to the view that ideal sizes have been reached and at times exceeded.”
